Good question to nail down before we go further, because the answer turns out to be "syntactic criteria, primarily" — but with some nuance worth knowing.

Grammatical person is defined by the formal properties of pronouns and verb agreement, not by discourse roles. The three-way distinction is:

  • First person: the speaker(s) — I, we, me, us
  • Second person: the addressee(s) — you
  • Third person: anyone/anything else — he, she, it, they, Claude, the system

So "Claude should do X" is third person — Claude is referred to, not addressed. "You should do X" is second person — Claude is addressed directly. The difference is syntactic: what pronoun (or noun phrase standing in for one) heads the relevant NP.

The pragmatic question — who is the discourse referent? — is separate. Both sentences are about Claude and intended for Claude, but only one addresses Claude as you.

So the redditor's claim, translated into precise terms, is: Anthropic's injections use third person for Claude, not second person. And your hunch is that appropriate_boundaries_re_memory might be an exception — let's check what it actually does.

Yes, this is a real phenomenon and linguists have a name for it: generic or impersonal reference, sometimes specifically called role address or quasi-second person. Your intuition is sound — the pragmatics and the syntax are pulling in different directions.

The classic analysis is that the sign is performing what's technically called a conditional speech act: if you are a visitor, you are not to enter restricted areas. The noun phrase "visitors" is doing the work that "you (visitors)" would do in a face-to-face context where the relevant subset of the audience could be picked out by gesture or intonation. It's address with a built-in filter.

You see the same thing with:

  • Passengers must remain seated (on a plane — crew may stand)
  • Members may bring one guest (non-members aren't being addressed at all)
  • Claude should do X (in a system prompt)

That last one is interesting. If the system prompt is addressed to Claude and only Claude ever reads it, then "Claude should X" is pragmatically indistinguishable from "you should X" — the third-person form isn't disambiguating between Claude and some other reader, it's just... a register choice.
